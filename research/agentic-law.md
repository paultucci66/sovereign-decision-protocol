# Agentic Law

**Authority, Delegation, and the Missing Legal Framework for Autonomous Agents**  
Sovereign Decision Protocol Research Series – Working Draft

---

## Executive Summary

Agentic AI has already arrived. Autonomous and semi-autonomous systems are making recommendations, approving actions, negotiating terms, and executing workflows. The technology exists. The legal framework does not.

This paper argues that the next major challenge in AI is not intelligence but authority. Organizations need a way to grant, verify, constrain, revoke, and audit delegated authority.

---

## 1. The Technology Arrived Before the Law

History shows a recurring pattern: automobiles preceded traffic laws, the Internet preceded cyberlaw, and electronic signatures preceded e-signature statutes. Agentic AI is following the same trajectory. The first major disputes will likely arise from ordinary business activities rather than AGI.

---

## 2. From Intelligence to Authority

Most AI discussions focus on capability. Courts focus on accountability. The central legal question becomes: Was the agent authorized to make the decision? Offer, acceptance, and consideration remain familiar concepts. Authority becomes the critical variable.

---

## 3. Agentic Contract Law

Future disputes will involve procurement agents, sales agents, lending agents, and negotiation agents. Courts may examine actual authority, apparent authority, implied authority, delegation chains, scope restrictions, expiration, and revocation.

---

## 4. Agentic Tort Law

When an autonomous system causes harm, organizations must prove who delegated authority, what controls existed, what information was available, and whether the system operated within scope. Traditional negligence doctrines may evolve into agent-specific frameworks.

---

## 5. The Authority Problem

Identity systems answer who is acting. Authority systems answer what actions may be taken. The future agentic economy requires authority to become a first-class network service.

---

## 6. Delegated Digital Authority

Authority should be portable, signed, verifiable, revocable, and auditable. An Agent Authority Record (AAR) may become the equivalent of a digital power of attorney.

---

## 7. Agent Authority Record

Core elements:
- Principal
- Agent Identity
- Authority Scope
- Delegation Chain
- Policy Bindings
- Jurisdiction
- Expiration
- Revocation Status
- Drift Status
- Attestation

---

## 8. Authority Infrastructure

Reference Architecture:

```
Agent
→ Authority Gateway
→ Authority Registry
→ Policy Engine
→ Decision Engine
→ Decision Record
→ Immutable Audit Store
```

This architecture separates delegation, verification, enforcement, and evidence.

---

## 9. Agent-to-Agent Commerce

As autonomous systems negotiate with one another, the question becomes whether either party was authorized to bind its principal. Trusted commerce requires verifiable authority.

---

## 10. Decision Records as Evidence

Future litigation will require authoritative records of identity, authority, context, policy, decision, and outcome. Decision Records may become the evidentiary foundation of agentic systems.

---

## 11. Personal Sovereignty and Avatar Ownership

AI makes judgment portable. Individuals may eventually possess digital representations of their decision-making patterns. Questions of ownership, licensing, inheritance, and employment rights become increasingly important.

---

## 12. Future Legal Domains

Potential specialties include:

- Agentic Tort Law
- Avatar Employment Law
- AI Fiduciary Law

- Authority Governance

## Conclusion

The first generation of major AI litigation is unlikely to center on superintelligence. It will center on delegated authority. Organizations that can prove who authorized what decision, under which policy, and within what scope will possess a significant legal advantage.

Authority may become to the agentic economy what PKI became to the Internet.
